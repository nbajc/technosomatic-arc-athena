01. Artist Statement

Theoretical Ground

Merleau-Ponty Mulvey Kristeva Cixous Lacan Bollas Noble

Technosomatic. Two words fused into a single diagnostic instrument. The digital infrastructure that mediates human experience collides with the body — the flesh, the primary site of knowing. Between them: the unthought known.1 The body already knows what the digital age is doing to it.

This exhibition refuses the asymmetry that has defined Western visual culture for centuries. Breasts are everywhere. The phallus remains a taboo. Female objectification is so woven into culture it has become invisible — a background condition requiring no justification. Male objectification is actively disallowed, treated as transgressive, even dangerous.2

Technosomatic Cyberfeminism 2.0 reverses this mechanism. Not to equalize. To expose.

The female gaze becomes the instrument of rendering the male body into visibility — clinical, unblinking, and permanent.3 In this work, the gaze is not merely a critical position: it is structural. It is the apparatus through which the body is processed, translated, and returned as data, as image, as thing.4

The ten works in this exhibition move across media — digital print on canvas, holographic installation, video, and interactive gaze-tracking — to map a single inquiry: what happens when the female gaze becomes the instrument of rendering, and the male body becomes the archive?5 The body passes through procedures: photographed, processed through AI, printed, painted over, sliced into holograms, looped, projected. Each procedure adds a mark. Each mark is a form of knowledge the eye-tracker will confirm the viewer already knew.6

Technosomatic Architecture is the methodological core: AI deployed not as an aesthetic filter but as a phenomenological instrument — a device for measuring where the body's knowledge diverges from the mind's.7 The works do not depict this divergence. They instantiate it.

03. Interactive Installation

Move cursor — your gaze renders the image

Selection

Where you look becomes sharp. Everything else blurs. This is the operational logic of Selection: the female gaze renders the male body into clarity, one viewer at a time. The image is identical for every viewer. What changes is which part of it they choose to bring into focus.

The eye-tracker captures the gaze vector in real time; a Unity shader maps it to a sharp window within a blurred full-frame projection of the male nude. The technology makes visible what is always already operating: attention as an act of rendering, and rendering as an act of power.

Ten people. Ten selections. The gaze is never uniform, never predictable, never repeatable. The archive of where women look is the work itself.

Mind Map: Gaze as Interface · LERATA Festival

Los Angeles, February 2016. Eye-tracking interactive installation premiered at LERATA Festival for Emerging and Innovative Art. The Tobii EyeX eye-tracker rendered a blank wall as the visualization of internal attention: where the viewer looked, the projector rendered light. The viewer's gaze was the instrument of creation. A decade of expertise in eye-tracking technology, interactive installation, and the gaze as primary artistic medium precedes Selection.

05. Artist

Nataša Bajc

Natasha Bajc is a Serbian-born, Los Angeles-based artist, architect, and researcher working at the intersection of the body, technology, and digital process. Her practice operates under the framework of Technosomatic Architecture — an inquiry into how the body is translated, processed, and returned altered through digital and material systems.

Bajc holds advanced training in architecture and computer science and has taught at the collegiate level in Los Angeles, with affiliations including SCI-Arc, UCLA, and USC. She is Co-Founder and CEO of Nexus Hestia Technologies, developing research at the intersection of artificial intelligence and spatial practice.

She gave one of the first TEDx talks in Belgrade in 2010, and participated in the Venice Biennale of Architecture in 2018. The Loop of Being was exhibited at the Beijing Biennale, 2025.

Her current body of work spans digital print on canvas, found object sculpture, holographic installation, large-scale interactive installation, and video. Bajc lives and works in Los Angeles.
