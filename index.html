01. Artist Statement

Theoretical Ground

Merleau-Ponty Mulvey Kristeva Cixous Lacan Bollas Noble

Technosomatic. Two words fused into a single diagnostic instrument. The digital infrastructure that mediates human experience collides with the body — the flesh, the primary site of knowing. Between them: the unthought known.1 The body already knows what the digital age is doing to it.

This exhibition refuses the asymmetry that has defined Western visual culture for centuries. Breasts are everywhere. The phallus remains a taboo. Female objectification is so woven into culture it has become invisible — a background condition requiring no justification. Male objectification is actively disallowed, treated as transgressive, even dangerous.2

Technosomatic Cyberfeminism 2.0 reverses this mechanism. Not to equalize. To expose.

The female gaze becomes the instrument of rendering the male body into visibility — clinical, unblinking, and permanent.3 In this work, the gaze is not merely a critical position: it is structural. It is the apparatus through which the body is processed, translated, and returned as data, as image, as thing.4

The ten works in this exhibition move across media — digital print on canvas, holographic installation, video, and interactive gaze-tracking — to map a single inquiry: what happens when the female gaze becomes the instrument of rendering, and the male body becomes the archive?5 The body passes through procedures: photographed, processed through AI, printed, painted over, sliced into holograms, looped, projected. Each procedure adds a mark. Each mark is a form of knowledge the eye-tracker will confirm the viewer already knew.6

Technosomatic Architecture is the methodological core: AI deployed not as an aesthetic filter but as a phenomenological instrument — a device for measuring where the body's knowledge diverges from the mind's.7 The works do not depict this divergence. They instantiate it.

Male Odalisque

The odalisque is a figure of the Western art historical archive: passive, horizontal, available — arranged for a gaze that is always assumed to be male. This work performs the inversion. The figure is male. The gaze that produces and holds the image is female. The historical pose — reclining, exposed, arranged — does not become ironic in this reversal. It becomes exact.

Amber and ochre push the body toward terracotta, something fired and permanent. The architectural cold of the background reads against the radiated warmth of the flesh. The pose is simultaneously vulnerable and unselfconscious — not performing availability but inhabiting it. The glasses insist on a particular Tuesday afternoon. That specificity — not the eternal nude but the actual person, on an ordinary day — is the sharpest edge of the work.

The paint applied over the print is not decoration. It is the mark of a second procedure: the image is made, then it is touched again. The body passes through two processes and is returned altered by both. What remains is not documentation. It is the record of a rendering.

Reterritorialization

The body as territory: claimed, mapped, re-inscribed. Reterritorialization is the Deleuzian moment at which a destabilized system acquires new form — not returning to its origin but settling into a structure it did not previously inhabit. This work applies that logic to flesh.

The digital process runs the body through a cartographic procedure. What returns is not the body as it was but the body as newly bounded — lines of demarcation that were always implicit made legible. The work positions the Technosomatic framework at its most spatial: the body as a site that can be occupied, re-drawn, and inhabited under new terms. The female gaze performs the survey. The canvas holds the resulting map.

Corpus I

The first state is not rest. The figure has contracted into itself — arms folded across the body, the frame occupied minimally, a teal ground accumulating beneath like something pooling at the base of a wall. The body is not collapsed. It is gathered. What reads as withdrawal is also preparation: the protective layer that precedes exposure, the anticipatory stillness of a body that knows what being seen costs and has not yet decided to pay it. The chartreuse ground presses in from all sides. The body does not push back. It holds.

Dimensions30″h × 40″w

Corpus II

The second state is the confrontation — but the confrontation is not aggressive. The figure is fully frontal, symmetrical, centered: nothing deflected, nothing withheld, nothing arranged to manage what is seen. The genitals are present. The torso is upright. The face is still. This is the body at maximum exposure, and it is also the body at maximum composure. The two conditions do not contradict each other. They are the same state.

The rendering process is most legible here: the AI-etched skin reads as geological, as something that has been worked at depth rather than surface. The body endures the procedure — the photography, the processing, the printing, the second touch of paint — with the same stillness it brings to being seen. The chartreuse ground presses in from all sides. The body does not accommodate it. It simply occupies the center. Power is not performed here. It is structural.

Corpus III

The third state is not conclusion but arrival. The figure is centered, frontal, knees open — a position that in classical anatomy diagrams marks the body's full disclosure, the pose of the medical examination or the spiritual surrender. Here it carries neither clinical detachment nor submission. The body is simply present: weight distributed, arms resting, face calm. The chartreuse ground that was hostile in Corpus I and contested in Corpus II is now simply a field the body inhabits. The transformation across the three works is not narrative. It is somatic. The body has moved through its own states and arrived at this one: open, meditative, complete.

Sub-item · 05a

Fig. 05a — Triptych · Corpus I, II, III · 30″h × 120″w installed · 2026

Triptych

Installed together as a single horizontal field, the three works have a clear center — Corpus II, the confrontation, the body at maximum exposure and maximum stillness — but no fixed direction of reading. The sequence can move outward from center to the closed anticipation of Corpus I, or outward to the meditative arrival of Corpus III. It can retreat from either edge back toward the center. It can reverse. The triptych format carries its art historical weight: devotional, sequential, cumulative. Here it is stripped of linearity. The chartreuse ground runs unbroken across all three, refusing to separate what the body inhabits from what surrounds it. What the sequence proposes is not a story. It is a field — with a center that holds, and edges that remain available in any direction.

MediumDigital print on canvas
Date2026
SeriesTechnosomatic Architecture

Odalisque Protocol

A protocol is a set of rules governing the transmission and formatting of data between systems. The Odalisque Protocol applies this logic to the pose: the conventions of the reclining figure — the angle of display, the arrangement of the body for reception — become legible as a transmission standard, a set of instructions for how the body should present itself to the gaze that receives it.

The work returns to the figure of Male Odalisque and treats it as a diagram rather than a representation. Where Male Odalisque is a specific person on a specific afternoon, Odalisque Protocol is the rule that governs such arrangements — the structural condition extracted from the singular instance. Together the two works map the relation between the universal convention and its particular instantiation in flesh.

RENDERED · 07

Fig. 07 — Restraint System · Digital print on canvas · 2026

MediumDigital print on canvas
Date2026
SeriesTechnosomatic Architecture

Restraint System

The most direct work in the exhibition. A restraint system is an engineering term: the assembly of components that holds a body in position under force. Applied to the body, the term makes visible what is normally invisible — that the body in display is already a body held, that the poses of availability and exposure are positions maintained, not natural states arrived at.

The digital process introduces its own restraint: the body processed through AI renders the flesh as a system of pressures and tensions made legible. What the eye reads as texture is the record of a body under load. The work is the darkest in the exhibition — not in palette but in implication. It makes explicit what the other works operate through: that the gaze is always also a form of constraint, and that to be rendered is to be held in place long enough to be seen.

RENDERED · 08

Fig. 08 — Otium · Digital print · 30″h × 40″w · 2026

MediumDigital print
Dimensions30″h × 40″w
Date2026
SeriesTechnosomatic — Rendered

Otium

Otium: the Roman concept of private leisure — time spent on oneself, away from public obligation, slightly idle, slightly shameless. The counterpart to negotium, the public sphere of productivity and duty. Otium is not rest. It is the condition of being unavailable to the demands of others while remaining fully present to oneself.

The work is a close-up so extreme the body becomes terrain: cracked mosaic, heat map, the skin read as geological surface. The digital process turns the explicit into the abstract and the abstract back into the intimate. What might be immediately legible as body becomes landscape, then resolves back into flesh. The body at its most private, in the middle of an ordinary afternoon. After the structural exposures of Restraint System and Odalisque Protocol, Otium offers a different register: not the body under the gaze of the work, but the body in the act of being — temporarily, privately, unobserved.

The Glitch of the Real

The glitch is not an error. It is the moment the system exceeds its own architecture — the instant when the body refuses the smooth operation of its own representation and something involuntary breaks through. In Lacanian terms, the Real is precisely what cannot be symbolized: the remainder that resists integration into the digital interface's promise of clean, managed experience.

The work presents the male body in the private act of orgasm — the body's primary glitch, the moment when the social mask is structurally suspended and flesh enacts what the digital register can only simulate. Presented side by side in the AI-tonal register of the series: the same event, two angles, the doubling producing a clinical distance that the content refuses to honor. The loop holds what cannot be held. The body twitches against its own containment. Witnessed by no one. Witnessed by anyone who stands in front of it.

TechnologyTobii EyeX eye-tracker
ProjectionShort-throw projector
SoftwareUnity · Custom C# shader
SubjectDeclan James Jones
Window80 × 80 px peephole
PrecedentLERATA Festival, 2016

Chambre Secrète

The secret room. The chamber that exists only when someone looks for it — and only shows what the looker chooses to find. The full image is present and entirely unavailable: a blurred, dimmed projection of the male nude, intact and inaccessible. The eye-tracker reads the viewer's gaze vector in real time and opens an 80-pixel window of clarity at the exact point of attention. Everywhere else: blur. At the point of looking: the image, sharp and immediate.

The Chambre Secrète makes the mechanics of voyeurism explicit. The desire to see produces the image. Without the gaze, there is no picture — only a blurred field that implies something it refuses to show. The viewer becomes aware of their own looking in the act of looking: the act of attending to the image is the act that constitutes it. The peephole is not a limitation. It is the work's thesis made spatial.

The image changes with each viewer. Ten viewers produce ten different Chambre Secrètes, ten different archives of where women look when they are given the instrument to look without consequence. The aggregate is never displayed. Each act of looking is private, temporary, and unrepeatable. The chamber closes when the viewer leaves.

03. Artist

Nataša Bajc

Natasha Bajc · Los Angeles · 2026

Natasha Bajc is a Serbian-born, Los Angeles-based artist, architect, and researcher working at the intersection of the body, technology, and digital process. Her practice operates under the framework of Technosomatic Architecture — an inquiry into how the body is translated, processed, and returned altered through digital and material systems.

Bajc holds advanced training in architecture and computer science and has taught at the collegiate level in Los Angeles, with affiliations including SCI-Arc, UCLA, and USC. She is Co-Founder and CEO of Nexus Hestia Technologies, developing research at the intersection of artificial intelligence and spatial practice.

She gave one of the first TEDx talks in Belgrade in 2010, and participated in the Venice Biennale of Architecture in 2018. The Loop of Being was exhibited at the Beijing Biennale, 2025.

Her current body of work spans digital print on canvas, found object sculpture, holographic installation, large-scale interactive installation, and video. Bajc lives and works in Los Angeles.
